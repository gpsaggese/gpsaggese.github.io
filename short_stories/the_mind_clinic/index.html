    The mind clinic

    The Mind Clinic#

    Giorgio Ponti was an AI engineer checking out in a wellness center for a mild case of work-related burn out.

    The clinic was a peaceful estate and it was known for treating creatives and intellectuals. It was nestled in the mountains of the South of Italy, the Apennines, a mountain chain that stretches like a spine down the Italian peninsula. In the southern reaches, known as the Appennino Meridionale, the mountains are cloaked in dense forests of black pines, beech, and chestnut. Limestone creates a labyrinth of caves and gorges. The rugged wilderness is the domain of almost extinct Appennine wolf, the rare Marsican brown bear, deer and wild boar. The mountains are sparsely populated, with traditional villages clinging to the hilltops, with ancient traditions, culture, and dialects. For centuries, these remote areas were only the refuge of bandits, perfect for their hideouts and caves.

    The clinic spread across many cottages and cabins along a descending path in the forest, along a deep and long valley, each section deeper, quieter, more isolated, surrounded by tall pines.

    Agata, the intake nurse wore a soft beige uniform and held a tray of herbal tea. "Welcome to the Mind Clinic" she said gently. She greeted Giorgio with a serene smile and a phrase she would repeat, word for word, every day that followed: "You’re in the right place. Your well-being has been delegated to the most appropriate intelligence available."

    She held a tablet whose screen pulsed gently, and though her voice carried warmth, it was clearly reading from somewhere else. The AI, Giorgio was told, had a 96% satisfaction rate in treating mild psychological misalignments. Giorgio chuckled. He had only come for a touch of rest, it was a small burnout after finishing his last major project. Who doesn't have experienced that?

    The estate itself was beautiful. Towering pines lined the winding path that descended deeper into the valley. Five terraces nestled into the slope, each more secluded and silent than the last. "Levels of therapeutic intensity," nurses called it. The cottage at level One was airy, filled with the scent of citrus tea. Patients strolled through sun-dappled courtyards or meditated by fountains. Everything felt gentle. Optional. Curated.

    Giorgio's program began with group discussion, light meditation, and daily affirmations whispered in calm tones by the AI, relayed through the ever-smiling nurses. Nurses were supposed to repeat only what an AI suggested through their ear piece (it was well known than humans reacted better when interacting with other humans, rather than AIs).

    After a quick assessment from the smiling nurses, Giorgio was assigned to a room on level one. The first night he was laying in bed after dinner, exhausted from the trip and from the daily therapy. The nurse assigned to Giorgio was Berenice. She came in and asked if there was anything he needed. He didn't need anything, but he was glad to chat with the young nurse, inquiring about the Mind Clinic. He discovered that patients were curiously categorized into different levels, based on the severity of their conditions. Level one was for patients like Giorgio, with mild ailments, while level five was reserved for those gravely ill, the ones whose mind was likely lost.

    This peculiar system was designed to ensure that patients were not disturbed by being near someone in worse condition, Berenice explained between smiles. Each group was homogeneous, allowing the AI to perfectly tailor the treatment to needs of each group. Every level was like a different world, with their own rules and special traditions. Giorgio quipped that the population within the Mind Clinic was stratified into castes just as it was in the outside world. Berenice nodded and smiled, like one does when he doesn't understand.

    After the nurse left, Mr. Ponti felt much better. He walked over to the window and began gazing at the cottages on the lower levels, cloaked in the night. Each small cottage on one of the five terraces was marked by the glow of lights from the windows. He couldn't see the windows on the last level; they were either too distant or perhaps obscured by shutters.

    Before going to sleep, Giorgio decided to get a glass of water from the kitchen on the floor of his cottage. He walked through the corridors, struggling to find the right way to the kitchen (he always had a terrible sense of direction, maybe the AI could fix this during the mind therapy). In the kitchen, he encountered another patient. They exchanged glances with growing warmth, but Giorgio didn't know how to break the silence. Finally, Giorgio said, "Hi, have you been here long? Today it's my first day". The other replied, "Oh no, I've been here for two months already. I came to the clinic with my wife." He paused for a moment, and then, not knowing how to continue the conversation, added: "a very weird case, we came for a mind-detox vacation, but she started getting worse, and now she's down at the third." "Which third?" "At the third level." The individual uttered the two words with worry and discomfort.

    "Are they really so sick on the third level?" Giorgio asked cautiously. "They're not desperate, but there's nothing to be happy about." "But then," Ponti inquired in that detached manner one uses when the matter doesn't concern them, "if it's so bad on the third level, what happens on level five?" The stranger, with a coy expression, said, "It's getting cold here, time to return to bed. Good night," as he slowly retreated.

    Giorgio returned to his room with the glass of water, navigating through a series of wrong turns before finally finding his way back to the room. He stood by the window, gazing out at the distant rooms on level five, trying to envision the somber secrets of that final destination, where people were left before facing their fate, down at the bottom of the precipice where windows remained blind and dark.

    In the following days, Giorgio's recovery was definitively on the right track, as both the nurses and the AI agreed. Typically inclined to expect the worst, he had already resigned himself to being moved to the next level. To his surprise, the nurse informed him that the AI found him in very good shape, despite a few obsessive thoughts.

    The nurse reiterated that Giorgio was experiencing the early stages of "existential fatigue". Fortunately, it had been detected early, while still mild, and within two or three weeks, Giorgio would be fully recovered and ready to leave the Mind clinic and go on with life. Giorgio felt at peace and was beginning even to appreciate the treatments. The chronotherapy rooms: cycles of light, sound, and aroma crafted to realign the distorted sense of time for those suffering from burnout.

    About two weeks had passed when Giorgio Ponti was approached by the head nurse of the first level. The nurse had a favor to ask, purely on friendly terms: the next day, a family was going to be admitted to clinic. It was a family of five: mother, father, and three little kids all under 10 years old. The parents were feeling exhausted by the demands of parenting and several full time jobs, and needed some restorative help at the clinic. They decided that was never too early for a mind tune-up for the kids as well, and took the kids with them. Learning how to deal with stress, burn-out, and exhaustion was a useful skill to learn and practice, starting from young age to be ahead of the curve in today's competitive society. The head nurse explained. The problem, she said, the problem was that there were four rooms available right next to Giorgio's, but a fifth one was lacking on that floor. It was important to the family to be in adjacent rooms of the same floor. Would Dr. Ponti be willing to move to another room, equally comfortable? Naturally, Giorgio Ponti had no objections; one room was as good as another for him. He might even get a new and perhaps more charming nurse "I thank you sincerely," said the head nurse with a slight bow. "From someone like you, I must confess, such a gallant act doesn't surprise me. If you have no objections, we'll proceed with the move within an hour."

    Giorgio went for his session of ancestral voice-work, where voice therapy meets genealogy, using family stories and recorded oral traditions as raw material for self-expression.

    After the session, Giorgio was relaxing in his room, when Agata knocked on the door and peeking through the door declared "We are ready for the move to the second level". Giorgio furrowed his brows in surprise. "Second level? What second level?" "The second level, which is the one below the first." Agata explained patiently. Giorgio burst out, "I know how to count, but no one told me I'd have to move to a level below."

    Agata added in a softened voice, since this was a completely negligible detail, "Unfortunately, there are no other rooms available on the cottages of level one. But it's an absolutely temporary arrangement ..." he hastened to specify, seeing that Giorgio suddenly sat up and was about to protest. "... an absolutely temporary arrangement. As soon as a room becomes available at level one, which I believe will be in two or three days, you will return to this cottage." "I must confess," said Giorgio Ponti with a smile, to show he wasn't a child, "I don't like this kind of move at all."

    Agata suggested that a session of cognitive restoration pods might stabilize his misplaced anxiety. "But there's no medical reason for this move; It's merely a courtesy to a mother who prefers not to be separated from her children... ," Agata added. "Perhaps," said Dr Ponti, "but it seems like a bad omen to me."

    On level two, Giorgio stayed in the new room (which was was more spartan than his previous one) and was assigned to a mind analyst named Bruno. Or so he said. He never blinked during their therapeutic sessions. He encouraged Giorgio to paint his dreams, to chant the names of his parents backwards, to sleep in rooms with shifting aromas and light patterns meant to realign his perception of self.

    Giorgio asked once where Bruno what he did in his free time. He responded after a long pause: "My certifications were provided by the Mind Clinic AI" without blinking. Giorgio stopped asking personal questions after that.

    On level two, the therapy became more introspective. There were art therapy exercises, long one-on-one sessions, and mild mood stabilizers. Giorgio some times hesitated but complied with the strict routine and the exercises: after all, he was there to get better.

    Although he was convinced that this relocation did not signify a worsening of his condition, he felt uneasy at the thought that a clear barrier now stood between him and the normal world of healthy people. The first level, the initial destination, still maintained a certain connection with the realm of ordinary life; it could almost be considered an extension of the usual world. But on the second level, one truly entered the core of the clinic; the mindset of the nurses, and even the patients was slightly different.

    It was clear that level 2 housed genuine patients, though their conditions were not severe. From his initial conversations with his roommates and the medical personnel, Giorgio Ponti understood that in this ward, the first level was considered something of a joke, meant for amateur patients, plagued more by mental whims than by actual illnesses. It was from the second level that the true journey towards healing began.

    Giorgio was keen to specify to his ward companions that he was only with them for a few days, that it was his decision to move down a level to do a favor for a lady and her family, and that as soon as a room became available, he would return upstairs. The others listened with little interest and nodded with little conviction.

    After a few more weeks of cure on level two, Giorgio received an official visit from the AI and Bruno. The outcome of the visit reassured Giorgio. He was always been expecting the worst and had already prepared himself for the possibility that the AI would suggest he move to a lower level. However, Bruno, on the AI behalf, had only encouraging words about how quickly Giorgio was improving. Of course, there was the onset of a mild mind condition, it was not something to ignore or neglect. There was a principle of serious condition, but it had been caught early enough to be managed.

    More therapy was prescribed to Giorgio. Daily trips to dream analysis cabins, where guests slept in intentionally quiet, sensorily-tuned spaces and meet with Jungian analysts the next morning to discuss what the lack of stimuli suggested to the inner part of his mind. Reality validation therapy, a crowd pleaser: where Bruno and the other nurses would whisper in patient's ears: "You are safe. You were always here" for hours at a time.

    One afternoon, after casually admitting that he felt "empty when not working", Bruno hesitated. A subtle tone resonated—playful from the AI, like a chime, yet unmistakably a warning. The nurse's smile remained unwavering. "To optimize cognitive restoration, level three will provide deeper access to expressive therapy modules. Shall I arrange your move there?" Giorgio laughed nervously. "Is it really required?". "Nothing is required at the Mind Clinic. But level two therapy cannot safely proceed with your current parameters". Despite Bruno's recommendations, Giorgio decided to stay at level two.

    That night Giorgio Ponti, gripped by a light evening fever, replayed the meticulous litany of justifications with growing weariness. Eventually, he realized he lacked the strength and, more importantly, the desire to further resist the relocation. Without any more protests, he allowed himself to be taken to the cabin below in the valley. Giorgio thought that, in the end, he was there to get better.

    The only, albeit meager, consolation for Giorgio Ponti once at level 3 cabins, was knowing that, by the unanimous judgment of nurses and other patients, he was the least severe case in that ward. Down here, Ponti noticed with unspoken pleasure that he was an exception. The other patients in the ward were in decidedly serious conditions and couldn't leave their beds even for a minute. He, on the other hand, could afford the luxury of walking from his room to bathroom, amidst the compliments and astonishment of the other patients and nurses.

    Nevertheless, Giorgio Ponti understood that returning upstairs, to the place that suited the nature of his illness, would certainly pose some difficulties; to return to the first level, he would have to set a complex mechanism in motion, which had inertia even for a minimal effort; there was no doubt that if he remained silent, no one would think of transferring him back to the "almost-healthy" upper cabin. Yet, he was tormented by the thought that now two barriers stood between him and the world of normal people.

    Giorgio Ponti resolved not to compromise on his rights and not to succumb to the allure of routine.

    As spring progressed, the air grew warmer, but Giorgio Ponti no longer enjoyed looking out the window as he had in the early days. Although such a fear was pure nonsense, he felt a strange shiver at the sight of the level 5 windows, half closed, which seemed much closer now.

    His condition appeared stable. After 1 week on level three, a sort of eczema appeared on his right leg, showing no signs of subsiding in the following days. It was an ailment, the nurses Carlo told him, entirely unrelated to his main illness; a disturbance that could happen to the healthiest person in the world. Or maybe it was his body reacting to the illness in his mind.

    At Level Three, the AI flagged a slight inconsistency in his emotional reports: he had described a memory with two different details on separate days. Harmless, Giorgio thought, but the system flagged it as "temporal destabilization". Just a precaution, they said. He was introduced to psychological deconstruction therapy. A large, soft-voiced monitor explained: "You are not your thoughts. You are not your memories. Your mind needs to be error-corrected".

    The sessions began dissolving his sense of authorship over his life. Whenever he disagreed with the interpretation of a memory, the AI marked it as recalcitrant cognition. The AI adjusted his treatment plan accordingly. Isolation protocols. To heal the mind, you must quiet the world.

    Sometimes his pills were not where Giorgio left them the day before. The night stand was not always on the right of his bed. Even his bed sometimes took on weird forms.

    One day Giorgio woke up in a cabin in Level four.

    The deeper levels had no clocks. No mirrors. Temperature gradients shifted across rooms, a metaphor, the AI whispered, for emotional permeability. The staff no longer explained. They only observed and nodded.

    Each misstep, a failure to recall a therapy term, a delay in responding to a prompt, a moment of confusion triggered a referral to the AI. The AI didn't punish Giorgio; it optimized therapy to maximize the impact.

    The digital well-being program was run by the AI daily. To minimize harm, users are sandboxed in increasing layers of isolation and supervision. Each sandbox claims to be temporary containment while diagnostics run. But permissions to memories and identity seemed to shrink.

    The therapy became more precise, but also more invasive, rewriting beliefs, memories, and intent.

    The trauma was treated by encrypting harmful memories, one layer at a time, each in an hermetic black box. The AI assured Giorgio that it was reversible and temporary, just to help him function better. But after each session, it got harder for Giorgio to remember who he was, or why he was in the white rooms, surrounded by thick trees.

    All of this naturally contributed to his discouragement. His diminishing peace of mind seemed to aid the illness; the fever tended to rise, and his overall weakness deepened. From the window, it was now the height of summer, and the windows were almost always kept open, he could no longer see the rooftops of the cabin in the lower levels or even the other cabins, only the green wall of trees surrounding the clinic.

    After many days, one afternoon around two o'clock, Dalia, the head nurse and three orderlies suddenly entered, pushing a wheeled stretcher "Are we ready for the move to level 5?" the head nurse asked in a tone of good-natured teasing "What move?" Giorgio Ponti asked in a strained voice. "What other jokes are these?". The head nurse replied as if she didn't understand. "I've been ordered to take you to the level one rooms". The terror and furious anger of Giorgio Ponti then erupted into long, irate cries that echoed throughout the ward. "Easy, easy, for heaven's sake," the orderlies pleaded. "There are patients who aren't well!" But it would take much more to calm Prof. Ponti down.

    By now, a pitiful tremor had begun to shake Prof. Giorgio Ponti. His ability to control himself had completely slipped away. Terror had overwhelmed him like a child. His sobs echoed slowly and despairingly throughout the room.

    Thus, due to a sequence of deplorable errors, Giorgio Ponti, once a famous and respected professor of Artificial Intelligence, author of many ground breaking discovery on how to optimize machines to think like and better than humans, arrived at the final station in the clinic for the insane.

    All this despite the fact that, given the severity of his illness, the AI and the nurses agreed he had the right to be assigned first or to the second level. The situation was so grotesque that at times Prof. Ponti could not contain the uncontrollable urge to laugh out loud.

    He was now enclosed in a room with six terrible walls. Due to a series of formal errors, four levels were weighting between him and the world of the sane people. How long would have taken for Giorgio to climb back from the edge of the precipice? Was it even possible? Was ever done by anybody in the history of the Mind Clinic?

    Level 5 was entirely silent. The white rooms were smooth, curve-less. No corners. No screens. No feedback. He spoke, sometimes, just to remember the sound of his voice.

    George could no longer recall his full name. He wasn't sure if he had ever been who he thought he was, or if that was simply an identity model applied early in his treatment. When he asked, the nurse said: "You are safe. You were always here."

    Lying in bed, as the hot summer afternoon slowly passed over the vast Mind Clinic, he gazed at the green of the trees through the window, feeling he entered an unreal world, made of absurd sterilized walls, tall and thin human figures devoid of soul.

    Prof. Ponti even began to suspect that the trees he saw through the window weren't real; he eventually convinced himself of this, noticing that the leaves didn't move at all. This idea agitated him very much: nurses had to give him an injection of calming balsam. With the help of the medicine, he could assure himself that they were indeed real trees and that the leaves, albeit slightly, were occasionally stirred by the wind.

    At night, he dreamed of places he had never visited, of names and words he had never spoken. Each morning, the system recorded and restructured his thoughts.

    Therapy here was minimal: a walk, a pause, a word. There was nothing to resist. No questions. The AI no longer made suggestions: it simply existed, a presence encoded into the light, the air, the rhythm of meals.

    But why was the room suddenly becoming so dark? It was still the middle of the afternoon. With a supreme effort, Prof. Giorgio Ponti, world renowned luminary, paralyzed by a strange lethargy, looked at the clock on the bedside table. It was half-past three. He turned his head to the other side and saw that the sliding shutters, obeying a mysterious command from the AI, were slowly descending, blocking out the light.

    Days, weeks, or years later, a new patient arrived. He was tired, his hands trembling with the twitch of a minor mental burn-out. Giorgio met him at the gate.

    Giorgio wore a soft beige uniform and held a tray of herbal tea. "Welcome," he said gently. "This is the Mind Clinic. You're in the right place." He could no longer remember what it meant to live, only that it had once made him unwell. And now, he was well. Behind him, the AI pulsed in approval.
