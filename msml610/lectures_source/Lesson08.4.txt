* Definition
- Causal inference question: _"what is the impact of $T$ on $Y$?"_
  - E.g., `is_on_sale` $\to$ `on_weekly_amount_sold`

- **Treatment for unit $i$** $T_i = 1$ if unit $i$ has received the treatment
  - E.g., `(weeks_to_xmas, is_on_sale)` = "apply price drop on a specific week"
  - Sometimes $T$ is denoted with $D$

- **Outcome** is the variable one wants to influence, $Y_i$
  - E.g., `weekly_amount_sold`

- **Unit of analysis** is the thing you wish to intervene on / treat, e.g.,
  - _"People who buy Y"_
  - _"When is the best moment to have sales"_

![](msml610/lectures_source/figures/L09/xmas_sales_df.png){width=80%}

* The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference
- _"You can never observe the same unit with and without treatment"_

- **Example**
  - Plot outcome `weekly_amount_sold` by treatment `is_on_sale`
  - Stores with price drops sell more (from 50 to 150)
    ![](msml610/lectures_source/figures/L09/xmas_boxplot.png)
  - Matches intuition: _"people buy more when prices are low due to sales"_
  - Magnitude of change seems too large
    - Maybe only larger businesses afford aggressive price drops?
    - Maybe customers buy close to Christmas anyway?
    - Observing both counterfactual situations would reveal the effect of price
      drops
    - Not possible due to fundamental problem of causal inference

* Causal Model
- A **causal model** is a series of assignments $\leftarrow$
  - Causality is non-reversible; you can't rearrange the equation

::: columns
:::: {.column width=50%}
- **Example**
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$
  where:
  - $u_t, u_y$ are exogenous variables (outside the model)
    - Account for everything not covered by model variables
  - Treatment $T$ and $u_y$ cause outcome $Y$
  - $T$ and $Y$ are endogenous to the model

* Applying a Treatment to Sales Example
- Ask _"what would happen to the outcome $Y$ if I were to set the treatment to
  $t_0$?"_
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow t_0 \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$
  - The intervention is represented by the $do(.)$ operator, e.g., $do(T = t_0)$

- Note that
  $$\EE[AmountSold | isOnSales = 1] \ne \EE[AmountSold | do(IsOnSales = 1)$$
  - The first is what happens to businesses that make a price cut (e.g., a big
    business)
    - You are "selecting" the subsample of businesses that actually cut prices
  - The second is that happens if you force every business to engage in sales

* Individual treatment effect
- One can use the $do(.)$ operator to express the impact of the treatment on the
  outcome for an individual unit $i$
  $$\tau_i = Y_i|do(T=t_1) - Y_i|do(T=t_0)$$
- The effect $\tau_i$ of going from treatment $t_0$ to $t_1$ for unit $i$ is the
  difference in the outcome of that unit under $t_1$ compared to $t_0$
  - E.g., $AmountSold_i|do(IsOnSales=1) - AmountSold_i|do(IsOnSales=0)$

- You can only observe one term due to the fundamental problem of causal
  inference
  - You can represent it in theory, but you can't recover it from data

* Potential outcomes
- Aka "counterfactual"

- The do operator can represent "the unit $i$ outcome would be $Y$ if the
  treatment is set to $t$"
  $$Y_{t,i} = Y(t)_i = Y_i|do(T_i = t)$$

- If the treatment is binary, you can use $Y_{0,i}$ (without the treatment) vs
  $Y_{1,i}$ (with the treatment)
  - One is "factual", i.e., observable
  - The other is "counterfactual"

* Consistency of the treatment
- There are no hidden multiple versions of the treatment
  - E.g., a drug coming in multiple dosages, but it's treated as binary
  - E.g., in "help from a financial planner", is "help" a one-time consultation
    or regular advice?

* No interference
- Aka "Stable unit of treatment value (SUTVA)"
- The effect of one unit is not influenced by the treatment of other units (if
  there are spillovers or network effects)

- E.g., vaccinating one person makes other close people less likely to catch the
  illness even if they don't get the treatment

//* Bias
//- "Bias is what makes association different from causation"
//
//- The claim "cutting prices increases the amount sold by a business" can be
//  questioned with "those businesses that cut prices would probably have sold more
//  anyway, even with no price cuts"
//
//- More formally the argument refers to potential outcomes
//  - $\EE[Y_0 | T = 1]$ = what happened to the treated if they were not treated
//  - In other words, we are trying to estimate $\EE[Y_t]$ using $\EE[Y | T=t]$
//    but this is a "biased estimator"
//
//* The bias equation
//- The association before the treatment and the outcome is measured by
//  $$\EE[Y| T = 1] - \EE[Y | T = 0]$$
//  which is the average amount sold by the business cutting prices minus the
//  average amount sold by the business that didn't
//- Causation is interested in
//  $$\EE[Y_1 - Y_0] \def= \EE[Y| do(T = 1)] - \EE[Y | do(T = 0)]$$
//
//- Let's express the first as function of the second
//  $$z = \EE[Y| T = 1] - \EE[Y | T = 0]$$
//- Replacing the observed outcomes (it is the same since each individual has two
//  potential outcomes but only one can be observed and the other is missing)
//  $$Y | T=1 = (Y_1 |T=1) + (Y_0 | T=1) = Y_1 | T=1$$
//  $$... = \EE[Y_1| T = 1] - \EE[Y_0 | T = 0]$$
//- Adding and subtracting $\EE[Y_0 | T=1]$, reordering and merging expectations
//  $$E[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] = 
//  \underbrace{E[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1]}_{\text{ATT}} +
//  \underbrace{(E[Y_0 | T = 1] - E[Y_0 | T = 0])}_{\text{Bias}}
//  $$
//- Association is not causation, but causation + a bias term 
//- The bias is given by how the treated and control group differ regardless of the
//  treatment
//  - Many things you can't observe are changing together with the treatment
//  - The treated and untreated businesses differ in more ways than just cutting
//    prices
//- For association to be causation, treated and control units would have to be
//  exchangeable
//

//* Phases of causal inference
//- A causal inference problem is often broken down into two steps
//  1. Identification
//     - Express the causal quantity of interest in terms of observable data
//     - Remove the bias, making treated and untreated comparable, so that all
//       the difference can be attributed to the treatment effect
//       $$\EE[Y_0 | T=0] = \EE[Y_0 | T=1]$$
//     - You need to know how the treatment was distributed or assigned
//     - This is why data alone (observational) cannot answer causal questions
//  2. Estimation
//     - Use data to estimate the causal quantity identified in 1.
//       $$te = \EE[Y|T=1] - \EE[Y|T=0]$$
//
//* Randomization removes bias
//- Randomization of treatment forces independence to hold and removes bias
//
//- Assume you can randomize the treatment
//  \begin{align*}
//  & IsOnSales \leftarrow rand(t) \\
//  & AmountSold \leftarrow f_y(IsOnSales, u_y) \\
//  \end{align*}
//- The dependency to $u_t$ disappears since the assignment mechanism of the
//  treatment is fully known
//- Treatment becomes independent from potential outcomes
