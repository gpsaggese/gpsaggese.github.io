* Definition
- Causal inference question: "what is the impact of $T$ on $Y$?"
  - E.g., `is_on_sale` -> `on_weekly_amount_sold`?

- Unit of analysis = the thing you wish to intervene on / treat, e.g.,
  - _"People who buy"_
  - _"When is the best moment to have sales"_

- _"Treatment for unit $i$"_ $T_i = 1$ if object / unit $i$ has received the
  treatment
  - E.g., `(weeks_to_xmas, is_on_sale)` = "apply price drop on a specific week"
- Sometimes $T$ is denoted with $D$

- Outcome = the variable one wants to influence, $Y_i$
  - E.g., `weekly_amount_sold`

* The fundamental problem of causal inference
- "You can never observe the same unit with and without treatment"

- If you plot the outcome `weekly_amount_sold` by the treatment `is_on_sale`
  you see that the stores that dropped price sell a lot more (from 50 to 150)

- This matches the intuition "people buy more when prices are low due to sales"

- The magnitude of the change seem too large
  - Maybe only larger businesses can afford aggressive price drops
  - Maybe customers buy close to Christmas anyway

- If you could observe the two counterfactual situations, you will know for sure
  the effect of the price drops
- But this is not possible due to the fundamental problem of causal inference

## Causal models

* Causal Model
- A causal model is a series of assignments $\leftarrow$
  \begin{align*}
  & T \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{align*}
  where:
  - $u_t, u_y$ are exogenous variables (outside the model)
    - They account for everything not accounted by the variables in the model
  - The treatment $T$ together with $u_y$ causes the outcome $Y$
  - $T$ and $Y$ are endogenous to the model

- Note that because of the non-reversibility of causality you can't move things
  around the equal side

* Causal model: sales example
- Causal model for sales example
  \begin{align*}
  & IsOnSales \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & AmountSold \leftarrow f_y(IsOnSales, u_y) \\
  \end{align*}

- You could add a $BusinessSize$ in the model
  \begin{align*}
  & BusinessSize \leftarrow f_s(u_s) \\
  & IsOnSales \leftarrow f_t(BusinessSize, u_t) \\
  & AmountSold \leftarrow f_y(IsOnSales, BusinessSize, u_y) \\
  \end{align*}

- One can make an assumption of linear model for $f_y(.)$ and say
  $$AmountSold = \alpha + \beta_1 IsOnSales + \beta_2 \BusinessSize + e$$

## Interventions

*
- You can force the treatment $t_0$
  \begin{align*}
  & T \leftarrow t_0 \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{align*}
  and ask "what would happen to the outcome $Y$ if I were to set the treatment to
  $t_0$?
- The intervention is represented by the $do(.)$ operator, e.g., $do(T = t_0)$
