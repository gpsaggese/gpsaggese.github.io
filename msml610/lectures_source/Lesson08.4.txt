  - E.g., when is the best moment to have sales (i.e., `weeks_to_xmas`)?

* Definition
- **Treatment for unit $i$**
  - $T_i = 1$ if unit $i$ has received the treatment
  - E.g., `weeks_to_xmas` `is_on_sale`
  - Sometimes $T$ is denoted with $D$

- **Outcome** 
  - The variable one wants to influence, $Y_i$
  - E.g., `weekly_amount_sold`

- **Unit of analysis**
  - The thing you wish to intervene on / treat
  - E.g., _"people who buy Y"_

* The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference
- **The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference**
  - _"You can never observe the same unit with and without treatment"_

* Causal Model
- A **causal model** is a series of assignments $\leftarrow$
  - Causality is non-reversible: you can't rearrange the equation

::: columns
:::: {.column width=50%}
- **Example**
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$
  where:
  - $u_t, u_y$ are _exogenous_ variables (outside the model)
    - Account for everything not covered by model variables
  - Treatment $T$ and $u_y$ cause outcome $Y$
  - $T$ and $Y$ are _endogenous_ variables (inside the model)

* Applying a Treatment to Sales Example
- Causal model is
  $$
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$
- Ask _"what would happen to the outcome $Y$ if I were to set the treatment to
  $t_0$?"_
  $$
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow t_0 \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$
  - The intervention is represented by the $do()$ operator, e.g., $do(T = t_0)$

- Note that:
  $$\EE[AmountSold | isOnSales = 1] \ne \EE[AmountSold | do(IsOnSales = 1)]$$
  - The **first** is what happens to businesses that make a price cut (e.g., a
    big business)
    - Some business (e.g., big businesses) are "self-selecting" to cut prices
  - The **second** is what happens if you force every business to engage in sales

* Individual Treatment Effect
- Express the impact of the treatment $T$ on the outcome $Y$ for an individual
  unit $i$
  $$\tau_i = Y_i|do(T=t_1) - Y_i|do(T=t_0)$$
  - The effect $\tau_i$ of going from treatment $t_0$ to $t_1$ for unit $i$ is
    the difference in the outcome of that unit under $t_1$ compared to $t_0$
  - E.g., $AmountSold_i|do(IsOnSales=1) - AmountSold_i|do(IsOnSales=0)$

- You can only observe one term due to the fundamental problem of causal
  inference
  - Represent it in theory, but can't recover it from data

* Potential Outcomes
- Aka "counterfactuals"

- The $do()$ operator represents _"unit $i$ outcome is $Y$ if treatment is set to
  $t$"_ 
  $$Y_{t,i} \triangleq Y_i | do(T_i = t)$$
  - The first is "Rubin's notation", the second is "Pearl's notation"

- For binary treatment use $Y_{0i}$ (without treatment) vs $Y_{1i}$ (with
  treatment)
  - One is "factual" (i.e., observable)
  - The other is "counterfactual" (i.e., theoretical, not observable)

- There is a slight difference
  - $Y_t = Y|do(T=t)$ is "what a business would sell if it cut prices"
  - $Y|T=t$ is "what a business that cut the prices sold"

* Consistency of the Treatment
- There is a single version of the treatment
  - I.e., $T = T_i$ means only one thing

- E.g.,
  - A drug can come in multiple dosages: only one is used
  - Giving a discount coupon is treated as the value of the coupon is the same,
    it's given only once, in a way that is always equivalent
  - In "help from a financial planner", is "help" a one-time consultation or
    regular advice?

* No Interference
- Aka "Stable Unit of Treatment Value (SUTVA)"
- The effect of one unit is not influenced by the treatment of other units
  - E.g., there are no spillovers or network effects

- E.g., vaccinating one person makes other close people less likely to catch the
  illness even if they don't get the treatment

* Average Treatment Effects
- Assume you can see both potential outcomes $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$ at the same
  time (which is impossible)

- **Average Treatment Effect (ATE)** is the impact that the treatment $T$ would
  have on the average:
    $$ATE = \EE[Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}] = \EE[Y | do(T=1)] - \EE[Y | do(T=0)]$$
  - E.g., average impact of price cuts on amount sold:
    $$ATE = \EE[AmountSold_{1i} - AmountSold_{0i}]$$

- **Conditional Average Treatment Effects (CATE)** is the impact of the treatment
  on the units in a group defined by $X=x$
  $$CATE = \EE[(Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}) | X=x]$$
  - E.g., the impact of having sales during the week of Christmas:
    $$CATE = \EE[AmountSold_{1i} - AmountSold_{0i} | weeksToXmas = 0]$$
  - It's ATE but on a subgroup with a certain characteristics

- **Average Treatment effect on the Treated (ATT)** is the impact of the
  treatment on the units that got the treatment:
  $$ATT = \EE[(Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}) | T=1]$$
  - You can define it, but not measure it
  - E.g., how much sales increase for the business engaged in price cuts:
    $$ATT = \EE[(AmountSold_{1i} - AmountSold_{0i}) | IsOnSales=1]$$

- We can define these quantities, but not measure it directly (because we can't
  observe both potential outcomes $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$ at the same)

* Bias
- Bias is what makes association different from causation

- The claim _"cutting prices increases the amount sold by a business"_ can be
  questioned with:
  - _"Those businesses that cut prices could probably have sold more anyway, even
    with no price cuts"_

- More formally the argument refers to potential outcomes
  - We are trying to estimate $\EE[Y_t] \triangleq \EE[Y|do(T=t)]$ using $\EE[Y | T=t]$
    but this is a "biased estimator" (i.e., an incorrect one)

* The Bias Equation
- **Causal inference** is interested in:
  \begingroup
  \small
  $$\EE[Y_1 - Y_0] = \EE[Y | do(T = 1)] - \EE[Y | do(T = 0)]$$
  \endgroup
- **What is measured** is:
  \begingroup
  \small
  $$\EE[Y | T = 1] - \EE[Y | T = 0]$$
  \endgroup

- Let's express one in terms of the other
  - Replacing the observed outcomes (each unit has two potential outcomes but
    only one can be observed and the other is 0)
    $$Y | T=1 = (Y_1 | T=1) + (Y_0 | T=1) = Y_1 | T=1$$
  - Adding and subtracting $\EE[Y_0 | T=1]$, reordering and merging expectations
    \begingroup
    \small
    $$
    \EE[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] = 
    \EE[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1] +
    \underbrace{\EE[Y_0 | T = 1] - \EE[Y_0 | T = 0]}_{\text{Bias}}
    $$
    \endgroup
  - Association = causation + a bias term 

* The Bias Equation: Remarks
- Association = causation + a bias term 
  \begingroup
  \small
  $$
  \EE[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] = 
  \EE[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1] +
  \underbrace{\EE[Y_0 | T = 1] - \EE[Y_0 | T = 0]}_{\text{Bias}}
  $$
  \endgroup

- Bias arises from differences between treated and control groups
  (besides the treatment)
  - Unobservable factors change together with treatment
    - E.g., there are confounders
  - E.g., treated and untreated businesses differ beyond price cuts
    - E.g., differ in size, location, day of the week on which they have a sale,
      cities they are located in

- For association to be causation, treated and control units must be
  **exchangeable**

* Simpson's Paradox
::: columns
:::: {.column width=50%}
- Trend appears in different data groups but reverses when combined
  - Relationship within subgroups can disappear or flip when aggregated
- Paradox occurs due to a confounder influencing the relationship
  - Aggregated data distorts results due to hidden variable

* Simpson's Paradox: Admission bias in University

- A university has
  - Two departments: Engineering and English
  - Male overall acceptance rate: $\frac{81}{120}$ = 67.5%
  - Female overall acceptance rate: $\frac{58}{120}$ = 48.3%

- Is a bias against females, due to lower acceptance rate?

- Break down data by Engineering and English

\begingroup \scriptsize
| **Department** | **Male** |  |  | **Female** |  |  |
|:-----------|---------:|--:|--:|-----------:|--:|--:|
|            | Applicants | Accepted | Rate | Applicants | Accepted | Rate |
| **University** | 120 | 81 | 67.5% | 120 | 58 | 48.3% |
| **Engineering** | 100 | 80 | 80% | 20 | 18 | 90% |
| **English** | 20 | 1 | 5% | 100 | 40 | 40% |
\endgroup

- At the department level:
  - Women have higher acceptance in Engineering (90% vs 80%)
  - Women have higher acceptance in English (40% vs 5%)

- Paradox reason:
  - Engineering has high acceptance; English has low acceptance
  - More men apply to Engineering; more women apply to English
  - Women apply more to harder department, making their overall acceptance rate
    appear lower, despite higher acceptance in each department

* Identifying the treatment effect
- We can't observe $\EE[Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}]$ since we can only observe one of the
  potential outcomes $Y_{1i}$ or $Y_{0i}$ and _not both_ 
- How can we recover causal quantities from observable data?

- The bias equation says that:
  $$E[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] = 
  E[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1] + 
  \underbrace{E[Y_0 | T = 1] - E[Y_0 | T = 0]}_{\text{Bias}}
  $$
- If $\EE[Y_0|T=0] = \EE[Y_0|T=1]$, i.e., the treatment and the control group are
  comparable (exchangeable) regardless of the treatment, then the bias term
  vanishes
  $$E[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] = E[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1] = ATT$$
- But it also holds $\EE[Y_1 - Y_0 | T=1] = \EE[Y_1 - Y_0|T=0]$
  $$ATT = ATE = \EE[Y_1 - Y_0]$$
  then the difference in means is the average causal effect

* Independence assumption
- The exchangeability property means that the outcomes are independent of the
  treatment, $(Y_0, Y_1) \perp T$

- $\EE[Y_0 | T] = \EE[Y_0]$: the treatment gives no information about the
  potential outcomes
- In other terms $\EE[Y_0 | T=1] = \EE[Y_0 | T=0]$

- In the business example, we couldn't tell apart businesses that chose to engage
  in sales from those that didn't

* Phases of causal inference
- A causal inference problem is often broken down into two steps
  1. Identification
     - Express the causal quantity of interest in terms of observable data
     - Remove the bias, making treated and untreated comparable, so that all
       the difference can be attributed to the treatment effect
       $$\EE[Y_0 | T=0] = \EE[Y_0 | T=1]$$
     - You need to know how the treatment was distributed or assigned
     - This is why data alone (observational) cannot answer causal questions
  2. Estimation
     - Use data to estimate the causal quantity identified in 1.
       $$te = \EE[Y|T=1] - \EE[Y|T=0]$$

* Randomization removes bias
- Randomization of treatment forces independence to hold and removes bias

- Assume you can randomize the treatment
  \begin{align*}
  & IsOnSales \leftarrow rand(t) \\
  & AmountSold \leftarrow f_y(IsOnSales, u_y) \\
  \end{align*}
- The dependency to $u_t$ disappears since the assignment mechanism of the
  treatment is fully known
- Treatment becomes independent from potential outcomes
