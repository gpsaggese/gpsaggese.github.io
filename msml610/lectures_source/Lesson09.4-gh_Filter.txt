* Tracking Objects
- Many problems can be formulated as **tracking objects**

- **Examples**
  - Navigation of aircraft, drones, autonomous cars
  - Robotics: arm kinematics to predict the position of joints
  - Sensor fusion: merge multiple sensor readings
  - Finance: predict economic variables
  - Computer vision: track moving objects across video

- **Kalman filter**
  - Used for state estimation in dynamic systems with noisy, uncertain
    measurements
  - Track state over time using model to make predictions and observations

* Some Guiding Principles
- **The world is noisy**
  - E.g., a car might swerve around a pothole or brake for a pedestrian
  - E.g., wind or ice might change the car's path

- **Sensors are noisy**
  - E.g., a kitchen scale gives different readings for the same object

- **Knowledge is uncertain**
  - E.g., you alter beliefs on how things work based on evidence and its strength

- **Combine past information and system knowledge** to estimate future
  information
  - E.g., if a car moves at speed $X$ at time $t$, the speed at time $t+1$ is
    $\approx X$

- **Data (even if noisy) > guess**
  - Never discard information, no matter how poor it is
  - E.g., two sensors, even if one is less accurate, are better than one

* Example of Estimating Body Weight
- **Goal**: estimate your weight over time while going to the gym

- Two possible approaches
  1. **Predict your weight**
     - Track calorie intake
     - Track energy expense
     - Compute expected weight gain
     - _Cons_: Difficult to track food intake and exercise accurately
  2. **Measure your weight**
     - Use a scale
     - _Cons_: Scale is noisy, water weight fluctuates, different clothes

:::: {.column width=50%}
- **Problem**: Prediction doesn't match measurements
  - E.g., at time $t$:
    - Estimate $\hat{x}_{t|t-1} = 159$ based on calorie intake and energy
      expended
    - Scale measures 164

* Example of Estimating Body Weight
- **What's the real weight?**
  - You need to blend prediction and measurement
  $$
  \text{estimate = 0.6} \times \text{prediction} + (1 - 0.6) \times \text{measurement}
  $$
  - Because of coefficient 0.6 you believe the prediction is more likely correct
    than the measurement
  - The prediction is always between the prediction and the measurement

- **Algorithm**
  1. Start with an initial guess
     - Assume it's correct for now
  2. Predict the next weight based on the model
  3. Measure the weight
  4. Estimate the next weight by merging values:
  5. Go back to first step

* Example of Estimating Body Weight

::: columns
:::: {.column width=50%}
- Two **problems**
  - The filter needs a correct guess of the weight change rate
  - The rate of change can vary over time

- **Solution**: estimate the rate of change from measurements
  - "Data is better than a guess, even if it's noisy"
  - Refine the estimate of the gain rate:
    $$
    \text{new gain = old gain + 0.3 (measurement - prediction) / 1 day}
    $$

- The "state" is given by `weight` and `gain_rate`
  - You need to predict and update both

* g-h Filter
- The previous algorithm is called **g-h filter**
  - $g$: scaling used to blend predicted state and measurement
  - $h$: scaling used to update the parameter of the system model based on the
    measurements

- g-h filters have different values of $g$ and $h$ to achieve different
  properties
  - E.g., pick $g$ to minimize the transient error when the derivative of the
    signal has a step (i.e., a discontinuity of the slope)

- Many filters (including Kalman filter) are just generalizations of a g-h
  filter

* g-h Filter Algorithm: Pseudo-Code

::: columns
:::: {.column width=55%}

1. **Initialization**
   - Initialize state of the filter
   - Initialize belief in the state
2. **Predict**
   - Use system model to predict state at next time step
   - Adjust belief to account for uncertainty in prediction
3. **Update**
   - Get measurement and associated belief about its accuracy
   - Use as estimate of the next state a point between estimated state and
     measurement
::::
:::: {.column width=40%}

* Interpretation of $g$
::: columns
:::: {.column width=50%}
- If $g = 0$:
  - Follow system model, ignore measurements

- If $g$ is small:
  - Follow model more than measurements

- If $g$ increases:
  - Follow measurements more, ignore prediction
  - Useful with accurate measurements, inaccurate model

- If $g = 1$:
  - Follow only measurements, ignore model
  - Too large $\to$ follow measurements, reject no noise

* Interpretation of $h$
::: columns
:::: {.column width=40%}
- Estimate model parameters from data:
  - Change of weight
  - Rate of change of measurements
  - Car speed on different terrains
::::
:::: {.column width=55%}
![](msml610/lectures_source/figures/L09.4.varying_h1.png)
::::
:::

- If $h = 0$:
  - Filter follows previous rate of change
  - Adapts slowly to signal changes

- If $h = 1$:
  - Filter reacts rapidly to significant signal variations

- If $h$ is:
  - Smaller $\to$ Smoother, slower rate adaptation
  - Larger $\to$ Faster rate adjustment

- **Note**: Incorrect initial state is similar to a changing state

* Control Theory Nomenclature
::: columns
:::: {.column width=50%}
- State space models developed in control theory $\to$ different nomenclature

- **System**: object to estimate/track

- **Filter**: algorithm to estimate system state

- **State of the system** `x`: current values of interest
  - Part of the state might be hidden
::::
