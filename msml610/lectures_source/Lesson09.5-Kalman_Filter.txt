* You Cannot Trust Either Source Alone
::: columns
:::: {.column width=45%}

- **Measurements Alone**
  - Sensor says "door"
  - Three door positions: 0, 1, 8
  - Cannot tell from single measurement which door it is
::::
:::: {.column width=45%}

- **Predictions Alone**
  - Dog moves right repeatedly
  - Position drifts over time
  - Uncertainty accumulates; eventually, no idea where the dog is
::::
:::

- Both sources of information (prediction and measurements) have limitations,
  but together they work
  - **Combine predictions with measurements**
    - Prediction: _"Based on movement, dog should be around position 4"_
    - Measurement: _"Sensor says wall"_
    - Check hallway: Position 4 is a wall
    - Agreement increases confidence
  - **After several cycles**
    - Only one position consistent with entire history
    - High confidence in dog's location

* Predict: Loses Information
- **What it does**
  - _"Dog was at position 3 and moved right 1"_
  - _"So dog is probably at position 4"_
  - _"But could be 3 or 5 due to movement error"_

- **Effect on belief**
  - Shifts distribution to the right
  - Spreads out probability mass
  - Uncertainty increases

- **Analogy**: Blurring a photo
  - Sharp image becomes fuzzy
  - Information is lost

* Update: Gains Information
- **What it does**
  - _"Sensor says door"_
  - _"Boost probability at all door positions (0, 1, 8)"_
  - _"Reduce probability at wall positions"_

- **Effect on belief**
  - Concentrates probability on matching positions
  - Peaks emerge in distribution
  - Uncertainty decreases

- **Analogy**: Sharpening a photo
  - Fuzzy image becomes clearer
  - Information is gained

* Bayesian Update Algorithm

- **Start with prior belief**
  $$Pr(\text{position}) = \text{uniform}$$
  - "Dog could be anywhere"

- **Get evidence** (measurement)
  $$Pr(\text{door sensor} | \text{position})$$
  - Likelihood of seeing "door" at each position

- **Update belief** using Bayes' rule
  $$
  Pr(\text{position} | \text{door sensor})
  = \frac{Pr(\text{door sensor} | \text{position}) \cdot Pr(\text{position})}{Pr(\text{door sensor})}
  $$

- **Posterior becomes next prior**
  - Cycle repeats with each new measurement

* Kalman Filter: Interpretations
- Extension of $g$–$h$ filters
  - $g$–$h$ uses fixed gains $g,h$ for position and velocity
  - Kalman filter computes adaptive gain $K_t$ from covariance $P_t$
  - Handles multidimensional states and general linear systems

- **State space model**
  - $x_t = A x_{t-1} + w_t$
  - $y_t = C x_t + v_t$
  - All quantities are Gaussian with given mean and covariances

- **Bayesian interpretation**
  - Prior and likelihood Gaussian $\Rightarrow$ posterior Gaussian
  - Recursively updates mean and covariance
  - Example: radar tracking of aircraft

- **Orthogonal projection interpretation**
  - Estimate is projection onto span of observations
  - $K_t$ minimizes $\EE[\|x_t - \hat{x}_t\|^2]$
  - Error orthogonal to measurement space (optimal)

* Kalman Filter: Bayesian Interpretation
- **Kalman filter** can be interpreted as based on Bayes theorem and Gaussians

- The **Bayes theorem** states:
  $$
  \text{posterior = normalized(prior $\times$ likelihood)}
  $$
- If the _prior_ and the _likelihood_ are Gaussian the _posterior_ is also
  Gaussian (conjugate prior)
  - Everything can be represented as Gaussians $\to$ encode PDF in terms of mean
    and std dev
  - Updating belief is equivalent to sum and multiplication of Gaussians

- **Algorithm**
  \begingroup \scriptsize
  ```python
  # Create prior (using current estimate and system model).
  prior = predict(x, process_model)

  # Create likelihood (using measurement).
  likelihood = gaussian(z, sensor_var)

  # Update belief using prior and likelihood
  posterior = update(prior, likelihood)
  ```
  \endgroup

* Sum of Gaussians
- Given two independent Gaussians $X$ and $Y$
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  & X \sim Normal(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2) \\
  & Y \sim Normal(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2) \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$

- The sum $Z = X + Y$ is $Normal(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with:
  $$
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & \mu = \mu_1 + \mu_2 \\
  & \sigma^2 = \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$

- **Interpretation:**
  - The mean is the sum of the mean (by linearity)
  - The variance always increases

* Product of Gaussians
- Given two independent Gaussians $X$ and $Y$
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  & X \sim Normal(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2) \\
  & Y \sim Normal(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2) \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$

- The product $Z = X \cdot Y$ is $Normal(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with:
  $$
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & \mu = \frac{\mu_1 \sigma_2^2 + \mu_2 \sigma_1^2}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2} \\
  & \sigma^2 = \frac{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2} \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$

- **Interpretation:**
  - Reduce variance by incorporating more information
  - If one Gaussian $X$ is narrower (more accurate), result leans towards
    $X$
  - If two Gaussians are similar (measures corroborate), result becomes more
    certain

* Kalman Filter: Classical Representation
- Typical symbols used in Kalman literature:

  \begingroup \small
  | **Symbol** | **Meaning** |
  |--------|----------|
  | $x$ | State |
  | $P$ | Variance of state (uncertainty, belief) |
  | $f()$ | System model |
  | $Q$ | System model error |
  | $z$ | Measurement |
  | $R$ | Measurement noise |
  \endgroup

- **Initialization**
  - Initialize state of filter: $x = x_0$
  - Initialize belief in the state: $P = P_0$

* Kalman Pseudo-Algorithm

- **Predict stage**
  \begingroup \small
  ```python
  def predict(posterior, movement):
    # Prior belief about state (mean x, variance P).
    x, P = posterior
    # System model: movement (mean dx) and its uncertainty Q.
    dx, Q = movement
    # Use system model to predict next state: x = f(x)
    x = x + dx
    # Adjust belief to account for uncertainty in prediction.
    P = P + Q
    return gaussian(x, P)
  ```
  \endgroup

* Kalman Pseudo-Algorithm

- **Update stage**
  \begingroup \small
  ```python
  def update(prior, measurement):
    # Prior belief about state (mean x, variance P)
    x, P = prior
    # Measurement z and belief about its accuracy R.
    z, R = measurement
    # Compute residual between estimated state and measurement.
    y = z - x
    # Compute Kalman gain K from prediction uncertainty P and
    # measurement noise R.
    K = P / (P + R)
    # Update state estimate.
    x = x + K * y
    # Update belief (uncertainty).
    P = (1 - K) * P
    return gaussian(x, P)
  ```
  \endgroup

- The Bayesian update is not apparent and it's replaced by Kalman Gain

* Kalman Gain
- Assume that:
  - Prior prediction $x \sim Normal(\mu_x,\sigma_x^2)$
  - Measurement $z \sim Normal(\mu_z,\sigma_z^2)$

- The mean of the posterior (using the multiplication expression) is:
  $$
  \mu
  = \frac{\sigma_x^2 \mu_z + \sigma_z^2 \mu_x}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2}
  = \frac{\sigma_x^2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2} \mu_z +
    \frac{\sigma_z^2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2} \mu_x
  = K \mu_z + (1 - K) \mu_x
  $$

- The Kalman Gain $K=\dfrac{\sigma_x^2}{\sigma_x^2+\sigma_z^2}$:
  - Is the scaling term that mixes the prediction and the measurement
  - Depends on the ratio of uncertainty of prior and measurement

* Multivariate State
- Often the state variable is multivariate, e.g.,
  - E.g., position and velocity of a dog (probably correlated)
  - E.g., height and weight of an adult (correlated)

- **Variance** is a measure of how a population varies, e.g.,
  - Variance = 0 means constant
  - Large variance means lots of variation

- **Covariance** are correlated variances
  - E.g., height and weight are generally positively correlated

- **Covariance matrix**
  - The diagonal contains the variance for each variable
  - The off-diagonal elements contain the covariance between $i$ and $j$
    variables
  - The covariance matrix is symmetric

- Correlation allows prediction
  - E.g., "as winter comes you predict you will spend more on heating your
    house"

* Multivariate Gaussian
- The marginal of a multivariate Gaussian is 1-d Gaussian
- Consider a contour plot (i.e., the intersection of a 2-d Gaussian
  $z = f(x, y)$ with a plane $z = c$)
  - The contour plot is always an ellipses

* Multiplying Two Multivariate Gaussians
- Given two multivariate Gaussians $\sim Normal(\vmu_i, \mSigma_i)$
- The product of the Gaussians is still Gaussian $\sim Normal(\vmu, \mSigma)$
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  \vmu &= \mSigma_2 (\mSigma_1 + \mSigma_2)^{-1} \vmu_1 +
    \mSigma_1 (\mSigma_1 + \mSigma_2)^{-1} \vmu_2 \\
  \mSigma &= \mSigma_1 (\mSigma_1 + \mSigma_2)^{-1} \mSigma_2 \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$
- **Note**: this is a generalization of the 1-d case to multivariate

  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  & \mu = \frac{\mu_1 \sigma_2^2 + \mu_2 \sigma_1^2}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2} \\
  & \sigma^2 = \frac{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2} \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$

  replacing:
  - $\sigma^2$ with covariance matrix $\mSigma$
  - Division with matrix inversion

* Core Insight: Power Comes from Correlated Structure
- Real physical systems couple variables through dynamics
  - Position evolves according to $x_{t+1} = x_t + \dot{x}_t \Delta t$
  - A change in $\dot{x}$ necessarily changes future $x$

- Correlation constrains what combinations are plausible
  - Large velocity with small position change is unlikely
  - Bayesian update favors consistent $(x, \dot{x})$ pairs

- No correlation means no information gain
  - If velocity comes from a different aircraft, combining it with position adds
    nothing
  - Mathematically, zero covariance implies no reduction in uncertainty

- Hidden but correlated variables create extra constraints
  - Velocity is not measured, yet strongly linked to position
  - Each measurement of $x$ indirectly informs $\dot{x}$

- Uncertainty shrinks because we intersect constraints
  - Measurement constrains $x$
  - Dynamics constrain relationship between $x$ and $\dot{x}$
  - Posterior covariance reflects both constraints
  - Multivariate filter exploits structure of motion
  - Structure converts noisy data into strong predictions

- Repeated updates amplify the effect
  - Each cycle tightens the $(x, \dot{x})$ relationship
  - Velocity estimate stabilizes
  - Position variance becomes much smaller than measurement variance

* Multivariate Filtering
- Covariance structure helps improve the estimate, e.g.,
  - You know an airplane direction can't change quickly
  - Knowing an approximate value for the velocity helps constrain possible next
    positions

- State variables can either be:
  - observed variables: directly measured by a sensor
  - hidden variables: inferred from the observed variables

- **Example: airplane**
  - You are tracking a plane moving in a direction (1-d problem)
  - At time 1, you are fairly certain about the position $x=0$, but you don't
    know the velocity
    - You plot position and velocity on an x-y plane
    - The covariance matrix between position and velocity is narrow and tall
    - It is narrow on the x-axis since you know that the position is around
      $x=0$
    - It is tall on the y-axis because of your lack of knowledge about velocity
  - After 1 sec, you get a position update of $x=5$
    - You can infer that the velocity is 5/s
    - The covariance matrix is then stretched diagonally

* Notation
- A Bayesian notation $a | b$ means _"$a$ given the evidence of $b$"_
  - The prior is $\hat{\vx}_{t|t-1}$, since you know only the information at
    time $t-1$, i.e., the previous state
  - The posterior is $\hat{\vx}_{t|t}$, since you know all the information at
    time $t$, i.e., the measurement

- **A simpler notation:**
  - Indicate the "prior" version of the variables (i.e., after the system
    update) with an overline (E.g., $\overline{x}$, $\overline{\bx}$,
    $\overline{\bX}$)
  - Omit the indices $t + 1$ and $t$ and use an assignment notation
    (representing "update in place" of a variable):
    $$
    x = x + 1
    $$
    instead of the mathematical notation using a different variable for each
    time step:
    $$
    x_{t+1} = x_t + 1
    $$
  - With this notation:
    - The prior is $\overline{x} = \hat{x}_{t|t-1}$
    - The posterior is $x = \hat{x}_{t|t}$

* Multivariate Kalman Filter

- With the previous notation:
  - State update: $\overline{\bx} = \bF \bx + \bB \bu$
  - State uncertainty: $\overline{\bP} = \bF \bP \bF^T + \bQ$
  - Residual: $\by = \bz - \bH \overline{\bx}$
  - Kalman gain:
    $\bK = \overline{\bP} \bH^T (\bH \overline{\bP} \bH^T + \bR)^{-1}$
  - Updated state: $\bx = \overline{\bx} + \bK \by$
  - Update state uncertainty: $\bP = (\bI - \bK \bH) \overline{\bP}$

- Where
  - $\bx$ and $\bP$ are the state mean and covariance
  - $\bF$ is the state transition function
  - $\bQ$ is the system error (i.e., the noise in the model assessment)
  - $\bB$ and $\bu$ model the control inputs to the system
  - $\bH$ is the measurement function
  - $\bz$ and $\bR$ are the measurement mean and covariance
  - $\by$ is the residual
  - $\bK$ is the Kalman gain

- Use the system model to predict the next state
  - When we multiply $\bF$ to $\bx$ we get the prior (i.e., the state before
    seeing any measurement)
- Form an estimate between the prior and the measurement

* Designing a Kalman filter

- The designer of the model needs to design:
  - The form of the state $\vx$ and $\mP$
  - The system model $\mF$ and $\mQ$
  - The measurement $\vz$ and $\mR$
  - The measurement function $\mH$
  - The control inputs $\mB$ and $\vu$ if there are control inputs

* Algorithm

**Initialization**
- Initialize the state of the filter
- Initialize our belief in the state

**Predict**
- Use process model to predict state at the next time step
- Adjust belief to account for the uncertainty in prediction    

**Update**
- Get a measurement and associated belief about its accuracy
- Compute residual between estimated state and measurement
- Compute scaling factor based on whether the measurement or prediction is more
  accurate
- Set state between the prediction and measurement based on scaling factor
- Update belief in the state based on how certain we are in the measurement

## Modeling

* State Space Systems
- A dynamic system is a system whose state (position, temperature) evolves
  over time
- Many dynamic system can be described as differential equations, since calculus
  is the math of changing values

- It is possible to convert a set of higher order differential equations into a
set of first-order differential equations
  $$\dot{\mathbf x}=\mathbf A\mathbf x+\mathbf B\mathbf u+\mathbf w$$
  where
  - $\mathbf A$ is the system dynamics matrix
  - $u$ are control inputs (e.g., pressing accelerator)
  - $\mathbf w$ is noise

- We are interested in finding a (recursive) equation that finds the value
  of $\mathbf x_k = \mathbf x (t_k)$ from the previous (discrete) state
  $\mathbf x_{k-1}$
  $$\mathbf x_k=\mathbf F\mathbf x_{k-1}+\mathbf B_k\mathbf u_k$$
  where
  - $\mathbf F$ is the state transition matrix (aka "fundamental matrix")

- State space methods because we express the solution of the differential
  equations in terms of system state

- There are methods for finding the state transition matrix
  - Compute the matrix exponential $e^{\mathbf A} t$ (many different ways to do
    it, despite numerical difficulties)
  - Linear Time Invariant theory (if time invariant): use inverse Laplace
    transform
  - Numerical solutions

* Designing the Process Noise Matrix
- Designing the matrix $\mathbf Q$ is very difficult
  - When you model a given process, you ignore certain effects and then you
    need to estimate the error
- E.g., model a thrown baseball
  - There are unknown factors (e.g., ball rotation, spin decay, drag, effects of
    wind and air density)

- If $\mathbf Q$ is too small, the filter is overconfident in the prediction model
- If $\mathbf Q$ is too large, the filter is unduly influenced by the noise in
  the measurements

## #############################################################################
## Non-Linear Filtering
## #############################################################################

* Optimality
- The Kalman filter is optimal (in a least square sense) under **assumptions**
  that
  - Everything is linear
  - Process and sensor noise is Gaussian

* The World Is Non-Linear
- The Kalman filter uses linear equations and can only handle linear problems
  - The Kalman filter is a mathematical model of the world
  - The output is only as accurate as the model of the world

- **The world is non-linear:**
  - System model can be non-linear:
    - Many physical systems are described by non-linear differential equations
    - E.g., a ball flying through air is affected by drag
  - Measurements can be non-linear:
    - To measure the height on a plane, you can measure the distance of the
      plane from the radar. Given the Pythagorean theorem, you get:
      $$
      x = \sqrt{\text{dist}^2 - \text{height}^2}
      $$
  - A Gaussian through a almost linear f(x) becomes quickly non-Gaussian
    (bimodal, skewed, etc)

- Rarely does a physical system have equations that can be solved analytically

* Nonlinear Kalman Filtering Overview
- Goal: estimate hidden state $x_t$ of nonlinear dynamical system
  - State dynamics: $x_t = f(x_{t-1}, u_{t-1}) + w_t$
  - Measurement model: $y_t = h(x_t) + v_t$
  where
  - $f(\cdot)$ and $h(\cdot)$ nonlinear
- Noise often assumed Gaussian:
  - $w_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf Q)$
  - $v_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbfR)$
- Exact Bayesian filter requires propagating full probability density
  - Generally intractable
- Nonlinear Kalman filters approximate the posterior
  - Maintain tractable representation of mean and covariance

- Different approaches
  - Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)
  - Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)
  - Particle Filter

* Extended Kalman Filter EKF
- **Core idea**: linearize nonlinear functions around current estimate
  - First order Taylor expansion of $f$ and $h$
- Replace nonlinear system by local linear model
  - Jacobians: $F_t = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, $H_t = \frac{\partial h}{\partial x}$
- Apply standard Kalman filter equations to linearized model
- Intuition
  - Approximate nonlinear curve by tangent line at estimate
  - Assume distribution remains approximately Gaussian
- Pros
  - Simple and computationally efficient
  - Use the linear Kalman machinery
- Cons
  - Accuracy depends on quality of linearization
  - Can diverge for strong nonlinearities

* Unscented Kalman Filter UKF
- **Core idea**: avoid linearization, approximate distribution instead
- Represent Gaussian by deterministic sigma points
  - Choose $2n+1$ points capturing mean and covariance
- Propagate sigma points through nonlinear functions
  - $x_t^{(i)} = f(x_{t-1}^{(i)})$
- Recompute mean and covariance from transformed points
- Intuition
  - More accurate to approximate distribution than function
  - Captures second order nonlinear effects

- Pros
  - No Jacobians required
  - Higher accuracy than EKF for strong nonlinearities
  - It is superior to EKF in almost every way

- Cons
  - Slightly higher computational cost

* Unscented Transform
- Unscented transform estimates the result of applying a non-linear
  transformation to a probability distribution characterized by a finite number
  of moments (e.g., mean and covariance)
  - E.g., compute the non-linear transform of a distribution, given mean and
    covariance estimate
  - Called "unscented" since "it doesn't stink."

- **Intuition**
  - Given a PDF $C$ with mean $\vmu$ and covariance $\mSigma$
  - Encode mean and covariance in a set of points (sigma points) that represent
    a discrete PMF $D$ with the same mean $\vmu$ and covariance $\mSigma$
  - Propagate the discrete PMF $D$ by applying the non-linear function $\phi$ to
    each point of the PMF
  - The mean and covariance of $\phi(D)$ approximate the mean and covariance of
    $\phi(C)$

* Unscented Transform: 1D Case
- The idea is that we need 3 sigma points for a 1-d Gaussian
  - One point for the mean
  - Two points around the mean
- Each point has a weight

* Unscented Transform: Sigma Points
- Consider a distribution $F$ and a non-linearity $\phi$
- There are algorithms to generate points and weights (given the mean and
  covariance of $F$) to evaluate mean and covariance of $F$ transformed through
  $\phi$

- In $n$ dimensions, we need $2n + 1$ points $\vx_i$ and weights $w_i^m, w_i^c$
  $$
  \begin{aligned}
  \sum_i w_i^m &= 1 \\
  \sum_i w_i^c &= 1 \\
  \mu(\phi) &= \sum_i w_i^m \phi(\vx_i) \\
  \Sigma(\phi) &= \sum_i w_i^c (\phi(\vx_i) - \mu(\phi))(\phi(\vx_i) - \mu(\phi))^T \\
  \end{aligned}
  $$
  - Note that selecting the sigma points has not a single solution

* Particle Filter PF
- **Core idea**: represent posterior by weighted samples
  - $\{x_t^{(i)}, w_t^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^N$
- Sequential Monte Carlo approximation of Bayesian filter
- Steps
  - Sample from proposal distribution
  - Weight by likelihood $p(y_t | x_t^{(i)})$
  - Resample to avoid weight degeneracy
- Intuition
  - Approximate arbitrary distribution, not restricted to Gaussian
  - Works for multimodal and highly nonlinear systems
- Pros
  - Very flexible
  - Handles non-Gaussian noise
- Cons
  - Computationally expensive
  - Particle degeneracy problem

* Intuition of Sampling Techniques
- Assume you have a distribution $X$ and a non-linearity $\phi$

- For every measurement:
  - Generate many points from $X$
  - Pass them through the non-linear function $\phi$
  - Approximate the result (e.g., compute mean and variance)

- **Problem**:
  - "How many points are needed to build an accurate output distribution"?
  - Even if $n$=500,000 points are enough for 1 dimension, for $k$ dimensions
    you might need $n^k$ points (curse of dimensionality)

* Generic particle filter algorithm
1) Randomly generate particle
  - Particles have all state variable that needs to be estimated (e.g.,
    position, velocity)
  - Each particle has a weight representing the probability that it represents
    the actual state of the system
2) Predict next state of the particles
3) Update weighting
  - Update the weighting of the particles based on the measurements
  - Particles that match closely the measurements are weighted higher
4) Resample
  - Discard highly improbable particle
5) Compute estimate
  - Compute weighted mean and covariance of the particles to get an estimate of
    the state and uncertainty

* Degeneracy Problem in Sequential Importance Sampling (SIS)
- Intuition: weights collapse onto very few particles
  - Most particles receive extremely small weights after measurement update
  - Only a handful meaningfully contribute to the state estimate
- Why this happens
  - Initial particles are uniformly distributed
  - Measurement likelihood sharply favors particles near the true state
- Particles with near-zero weight
  - Contribute almost nothing to expectations
  - Do not help represent the true distribution
- Geometric intuition
  - Posterior mass concentrated in small region of state space
  - Most particles lie in low-probability regions
- Consequence
  - Effective sample size becomes very small
  - Example: $5000$ particles but only $3$ with significant weight
- Interpretation
  - The approximation of the posterior is poor despite large $N$
  - Computational resources are wasted on useless particles

* Resampling: Core Idea
- Goal: focus computational effort on high-probability regions
- Strategy
  - Discard particles with very small weights
  - Duplicate particles with high weights
- Effect
  - New particle set better approximates posterior
  - Majority of particles lie in meaningful regions
- Role of prediction noise
  - Slight dispersion after duplication
  - Prevents particles from collapsing to identical copies
