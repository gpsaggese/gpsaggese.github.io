* Business model
- A business model describes how an organization creates, delivers, and captures
  value
- A business plan is a shared understanding of what a business model is

* The Business Model Canvas
- Osterwalder, 2010

- There are 9 building blocks showing how a company intends to make money

  1) Customer Segments (CS)
     - An organization serves one or more Customer Segments (CS)

  2) Value Proposition (VP)
     - An organization seeks to solve customer problems and satisfy customers with
       Value Proposition (VP)

  3) Channels (CH)
     - Value Propositions (VP) are delivered to customers through distribution and
       sales Channels (CH)

  4) Customer Relationships (CR)
     - Customer Relationships (CR) are maintained with each Customer Segment (CS)

  5) Revenue Streams (RS)
     - Revenue Streams result from Value Propositions offered to Customer Segments

  6) Key Resources (KR)
     - Key Resources are assets required to offer products and services

  7) Key Activities (KA)

  8) Key Partnerships (KP)
     - Some activities are outsourced and others acquired outside the company

  9) Cost Structure (C$)

## Customer Segments

* Customer Segments
- Any company needs customers to survive

- A company may group customers into distinct segments with common needs and
  behaviors
- A business model is designed around a strong understanding of specific customer
  needs

- Customer segments:
  - Justify a distinct offer
  - Can be reached through different Distribution Channels
  - Require different types of relationships
  - Have different profitability

* Mass market
- A large group of customers with broadly similar needs and problems
- Typically involves products that are widely used and have universal appeal
- Products are usually standardized rather than customized
- Focuses on high sales volume and low cost per unit
- E.g., consumer electronics, household appliances, and fast-moving consumer
  goods

* Niche market
- A specific, specialized Customer Segment
- E.g. car part manufacturers depend on specific automobile manufacturers

* Segmented Customer Segments
- Customer segmented with slightly different needs and problems
- E.g., a bank can distinguish customers worth up $100k and affluent clients
  worth more than $1M
- E.g., a manufacturing solutions company targeting 3 CS, i.e., watch industry,
  medical industry, industrial automation sectors

* Diversified Customer Segments
- Serve unrelated Customer Segments with very different needs and problems
- E.g., Amazon has retail business and cloud computing services (catering to Web
  companies with a totally different Value Proposition)

* Multi-sided markets
- Serve two or more interdependent Customer Segments
- E.g., a credit card company needs credit card holders and merchants that accept
  credit cards
- E.g., a company selling newspapers need reader base and advertisers

## Value Proposition

* Value Proposition: Definition
- The Value Proposition is the reason why customers turn to one company over
  another
- VP solves a customer problem and satisfies a customer need
- Product / service catering to a specific Customer Segment

* Newness
- VP can satisfy a new set of needs that customers previously didn't perceive
- E.g., technology related
- E.g., cellphones created a new industry around mobile communication

* Performance
- Improving product and service performance is a common way to create value
- E.g., PC sector (more powerful machines, more disk storage, better graphics)

* Customization
- Tailor products and services to specific needs of Customer Segments /
  individual customers
- E.g., mass customization

* Getting the job done
- E.g., airlines relying on manufacturers of jet engines, so they can focus on
  running airlines

* Design
- A product may stand out because of superior design
- It might be difficult to measure
- E.g., fashion industry, consumer electronics

* Brand / status
- Customers may find value in the act of using and displaying a specific brand
- E.g., wearing a Rolex watch signifies wealth
- E.g., skateboarders can wear the latest brands to show that they are "in"

* Price
- Offering similar value at a lower price
- E.g., no-frills airline (Southwest) enable low-cost air travel

* Cost reduction
- Helping customers reduce costs creates value

* Risk reduction
- Customers value reducing risks when purchasing products or services
- E.g., one-year guarantee
- E.g., service-level guarantee in outsourced IT services

* Accessibility
- Make products and services available to customers who previously lacked access
  to them
- E.g., NetJets popularized fractional private jet ownership
- E.g., Mutual Funds allow people with modest wealth to diversify investment
  portfolios

* Convenience / usability
- E.g., iPod / iTunes offered convenient buying and listening to digital music

## Channels

*
- Channels allow companies to communicate with and reach its Customer Segments to
  deliver a Value Proposition
- Raise awareness among customers about a company's products and services
- Allow customers to purchase products and services
- Deliver Value Proposition to customers
- Provide post-purchase customer support

* Channel phases
- Awareness
  - Raise customers' awareness about company's products and services
- Evaluation
  - Help customers evaluate a company's Value Propositions
- Purchase
  - Allow customers to purchase products and services
- Delivery
  - Deliver value proposition to customers
- After sales
  - Provide post-purchase customer support

* Channel types
- Owned channels
  - E.g., in-house sales force, web site
  - Higher margins but costly to put in place
- Indirect
  - E.g., retail stores owned or operated by the company
- Partner
  - E.g., wholesale distribution, retail
  - Lower margins but expand reach through partner strengths

## Customer relationships

* 
- Company establish different types of relationships with Customer Segments
  - Customer acquisition
  - Customer retention
  - Boosting sales

* Personal assistance
- Human customer representative during the sales or after the purchase
- E.g., point of sale, call centers, emails

* Dedicated personal assistance
- E.g., in private banking services for high net worth individuals
- E.g., account managers maintaining personal relationships with important
  customers

* Self-service
- Provide necessary means for customers to hep themselves

* Automated services
- Mix customer self-service with automated processes
- E.g., online profile allow to access customized services (e.g.,
  recommendations)

* Communities
- Companies maintain online communities to exchange knowledge and solve users'
  problems

* Co-creation
- Companies invite customers to write reviews creating value for other customers
  (e.g., Amazon)
- Engage customers to design new products
- Solicit customers to create content (e.g., YouTube.com)

## Revenue Streams

*
- For what value a Customer Segment is truly willing to pay?
- Revenue Streams can have different pricing mechanisms (e.g., fixed list price,
  bargaining, auctioning, volume dependent, market dependent)
- One-time payments vs ongoing payments

* Asset sale
- Sell ownership of a product

* Usage fee
- The more a service is used, the more the customer pays
- E.g., number of minutes spent on the phone
- E.g., a hotel charges by number of nights in a room

* Subscription fee
- Sell continuous access to a service
- E.g., monthly subscription to a gym or to a web-based game

* Lending / renting / leasing
- Temporarily granting rights to use an asset for a fixed period
- E.g., car rental

* Licensing
- Give customers permission to use intellectual property in exchange for a
  licensing fees
- E.g., patent / copyright in media industry and technology

* Brokerage fees
- Intermediation services performed on behalf of multiple parties
- E.g., credit card providers charge a percentage of transaction value
- E.g., real estate agents earn a commission when matching buyers and sellers

* Advertising
- Fees for advertising product, service, or brand
- E.g., media industry

### Pricing mechanisms

* List price
- Fixed prices for products and services

* Product feature dependent
- Price depends on the number or quality of Value Proposition features

* Customer segment dependent
- Price depends on the type and characteristics of a Customer Segment

* Volume dependent
- Prices is function of the quantity purchased

* Negotiation / bargaining
- Price negotiated between partners (with different negotiation power / skills)

* Yield management
- Price depends on inventory
- E.g., hotel rooms, airlines seats

* Real-time market
- Price is established dynamically based on supply and demand

* Auctions
- Price determined by competitive bidding

## Key Resources

* 
- Key Resources are needed to create and offer Value Proposition
- E.g., microchip designer needs human resources
- E.g., microchip manufacturer requires capital-intensive production facilities

* Physical
- E.g., manufacturing facilities, buildings, machines, point-of-sales,
  distribution networks
- E.g., Wal-Mart has a network of stores and logistics infrastructure
- E.g., Amazon has It, warehouse, logistics infrastructure

* Intellectual
- E.g., brands, IP, patents, copyrights, customer database
- E.g., Nike has brand
- E.g., Microsoft has IP developed over many years
- E.g., Qualcomm has patented microchip designs that yield licensing fees

* Human
- E.g., knowledge-intensive and creative industries
- E.g., pharmaceutical companies use experienced scientists and skilled sales
  force

* Financial
- Some business models need financial resources (e.g., cash, lines of credit,
  stock option pools for key employees)

## Key Activities

*
- Key Activities are required to create and offer Value Proposition and earn
  revenues

* Production
- Design, build, and deliver a product
- E.g., manufacturing firms
- E.g., Microsoft does software development
- E.g., Dell manages supply chain and builds PCs

* Problem solving
- Come up with solutions to individual customer problems
- Need knowledge management and continuous training
- E.g., consultancies, hospitals

* Platform
- E.g., matchmaking platforms
- E.g., eBay, Visa

## Key Partnerships

* Different types of partnerships
- Strategic alliances between non-competitors
- Strategic partnerships between competitors
- Joint ventures to develop new businesses
- Buyer-supplier relationships to assure reliable supplies

* Motivations for partnerships
- Optimization
  - It is illogical for a company to own all resources and perform every activity
    by itself
  - Economy of scale to reduce costs
  - Outsourcing
- Reduction of risk
  - Competitors can form alliance in one area and compete in another
  - E.g., Blu-ray is a disc format developed by consumer electronic
    manufacturers, which then compute in selling their own Blu-ray products
- Acquisition of particular resources and activities
  - Few companies own all the resources or perform all the activities required in
    their business model
  - Rather they rely on other firms to acquire knowledge, licenses, access to
    customers
  - E.g., a phone manufacturer may license an OS
  - E.g., an insurer may rely on independent brokers to sell policies rather than
    develop its own sales force

## Cost Structure

* 
- Business models incur in costs, e.g.,
  - Creating and delivering value
  - Maintaining Customer Relationships
  - Generating revenues
- Costs should be minimized in every business model
- Some business models are more sensitive to costs than others

* Cost-driven companies
- Create the leanest possible Cost Structure, using low-price Value Proposition,
  maximum automation, outsourcing
- E.g., no-frills airlines (e.g., Southwest)

* Value-driven companies
- Some companies are less concerned with cost implication and focus on value
  creation
- E.g., luxury hotels

* Characteristics of cost structures
- Fixed costs
  - Costs don't change despite volume of goods / services produced
  - E.g., salaries, rents
- Variable costs
  - Costs that vary proportionally with the volume of goods / services produced
  - E.g., music festivals have high variable costs
- Economies of scale
  - Cost advantages that a business enjoys as its output expands
- Economies of scope
  - Cost advantages that a business enjoys due to a larger scope of operations
  - E.g., the same distribution channels and marketing can support multiple
    products

# Competitive Analysis & Positioning (SWOT, differentiation)

* More on value capture
- You should not stress out value capture in the beginning
  - The amount of value you capture is proportional to the amount of value you
    create
  - You need to pass some of the value to your customer
  - If your product saves people \$100, but it costs \$100 then your customers
    are indifferent

- Tokens can capture value in many ways (discussed later)
- There are many pricing models:
  - Value pricing
    - Best approach
    - Estimate how much value your are creating for your customers and charge a
      part of that (e.g., 50-80%)
  - Transaction pricing
    - Every time you make a transaction, I get a cut of it
    - E.g., financial exchange
  - Recurring revenues
    - E.g., there is a data service and you get a value out of it so you pay by
      seat
    - Some customers are getting a lot of value, others are getting small value
    - You don't know who is making money so you can't charge it
    - That's why people have multiple price tiers
  - Services
    - Somebody is paying you for doing something
    - E.g., a plumber charging for time and materials

* Commodity pricing
- The service becomes "cost + epsilon"
- E.g., consulting
- Price competition brings down the price to raw materials and wages
- Even if you are creating value, you are not capturing it
- This is the best situation for customers

- E.g., the Internet creates tons of value but you almost don't pay for it
  - Internet is a commodity (like water)

* 5 ways to build a $100m business
- Hunt for 10,000 flies that pay you $10
- Photoshop charges \$100 and need \$1m
- Greenhouse, Salesforce (enterprise software): $100k people pay $1000
- Big game hunting
- Elephant hunting (e.g., Palantir): each customer pays $100,000k

* More on defensibility
- Competitors cannot easily enter the market or overtake your market share
- E.g., network effect
  - E.g., Facebook, Ethereum, Binance
  - The platform that everybody uses is difficult to disrupt

- Other sources of defensibility
  - Economy of scale
    - The R&D dept doesn't need to grow if you grow
  - Pricing power
    - You have the ability to squeeze your supplier, like others can't
  - First mover advantage
    - You are the first one to do something, so you build the relationships
  - Access to capital
    - Lots of business need working capital so they need to borrow money
    - It's easier for you to raise or borrow money (maybe because you have a
      great pedigree)
    - If you have strong reputation or pedigree
  - Access to talent
  - Execution
    - It is the weakest
    - You can outrun your competitors
    - I have ability to build faster, ship faster than my competitors

* Market structure
- In almost all industries there are companies with certain characteristics
  - Some companies own the user relationship
    - High value capture
    - Network effects
    - Brand
  - Some companies are integrators (they put things together)
    - Low value capture (they get squeezed between the extremes)
    - Few moats
  - Some companies own the tech platform
    - High value capture
    - High switching costs
    - Economies of scale
    - Technical barriers

- E.g., mobile
  - (Facebook, Apple) <-> Motorola <-> ARM
- E.g., auto
  - Tesla <-> Modine <-> Mobileye
- E.g., streaming
  - Netflix <-> Starz <-> AWS
- E.g., computers
  - Microsoft <-> Acer <-> Intel

- Even if you have the best content but you need to go through Netflix to
  distribute it
  - Netflix wants to commoditize everyone else
  - For this reason Disney started Disney+
- AWS copies / adopts open source projects and puts them on their platform

- Same phenomenon also in crypto
  - Own user relationship: Coinbase, Binance
  - Integrators: protocols (e.g., Uniswap)
  - Tech platform: Ethereum, Bitcoin

- Everyone tries to move up the curve
  - Coinmarketcap and Coingecko start adding the buy button to allow trading
  - Polkadot compete with other blockchains to have people build on top of them
  - Uniswap builds a web interface to interact with users
  - Binance builds a BNB chain 

- At the bottom of the curve, your users is who is close to you
