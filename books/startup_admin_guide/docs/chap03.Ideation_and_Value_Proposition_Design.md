* Value Proposition: Definition
- The Value Proposition is the reason why customers turn to one company over
  another
- VP solves a customer problem and satisfies a customer need
- Product / service catering to a specific Customer Segment

* Newness
- VP can satisfy a new set of needs that customers previously didn't perceive
- E.g., technology related
- E.g., cellphones created a new industry around mobile communication

* Performance
- Improving product and service performance is a common way to create value
- E.g., PC sector (more powerful machines, more disk storage, better graphics)

* Customization
- Tailor products and services to specific needs of Customer Segments /
  individual customers
- E.g., mass customization

* Getting the job done
- E.g., airlines relying on manufacturers of jet engines, so they can focus on
  running airlines

* Design
- A product may stand out because of superior design
- It might be difficult to measure
- E.g., fashion industry, consumer electronics

* Brand / status
- Customers may find value in the act of using and displaying a specific brand
- E.g., wearing a Rolex watch signifies wealth
- E.g., skateboarders can wear the latest brands to show that they are "in"

* Price
- Offering similar value at a lower price
- E.g., no-frills airline (Southwest) enable low-cost air travel

* Cost reduction
- Helping customers reduce costs creates value

* Risk reduction
- Customers value reducing risks when purchasing products or services
- E.g., one-year guarantee
- E.g., service-level guarantee in outsourced IT services

## Idea Generation and Validation Process

* Ideas
- Ideas should not be safeguarded, they are not like secret jewels
- You should tell everyone about your idea to understand which one is good
- Have lots of ideas and talk about them
- You need to iterate on the idea

- There are two ways to find an idea

* Top down ideation
- Look at the industry, what are the biggest problems in the industry?
  - E.g., blockchain privacy, transaction monitoring, tax compliance
- What is the total addressable market (TAM) for each problem?
  - What is the best possible outcome?
  - Maybe the only lesson to learn is when the TAM is too small
- How crowded is the area?
- Are there recent innovations that change the market structure?
  - E.g., zero-knowledge proofs
  - Read the prior art, get in touch with people
- For ideas that require behavioral change, TAM is likely a futile exercise
  - Airbnb: "How many people will decide to stay in somebody else's house?"
  - Uber
  - Twitter is about talking with strangers
- Behaviors change but that's difficult
- Use your judgement, napkin math
  - How many piano tuners there are in Chicago?

* Bottoms up ideation
- What's the area you know best? What can I do?
  - If you don't know any area well, then spend time in it
  - What feels for me like me the biggest pain point?
- You use your knowledge, your network, your relationships, distribution
  - Use your unfair edge

- The risk is to over-optimization because you know what you like

* What makes a good ideas?

1. A good idea solves a big, clearly articulable problem

- Big: solving a small problem becomes a feature in somebody else product
- Articulable: if you cannot define exactly the problem you are solving, but you
  think "this is really cool and people will like it"
  - E.g., "this is great since it's decentralized"
  - "Create a website that allows to showcase NFTs in a shareable way"

2. A good idea is defensible (it posses a moat)

- When you solve a problem, you want to be the one that capitalizes on your
  solution

3. A good idea is easy to explain

- If you can't explain, either it's not a good idea or you don't understand it

4. A good idea is a well-defined customer / user

5. A good idea has a "why now?"

- Why doesn't this great solution already exist?
- "If this was a good idea, somebody else would have built it" (like the
  Efficient Market Hypothesis)
- Sometimes you need a stroke of insights
- The most common cause for failing startups is founder breakup, nobody wants
  what you are building (this should be the default hypothesis)
- Why now?
  - Nobody has my background and you need that to solve this problem
  - Crypto really grew in the last few years and now people need custodial
    services
  - Academic papers have been written and we made a product out of them

6. Good idea has a strong business model

- High margin
- Strong capture value
- E.g., Linux created tons of value but didn't capture it
- Music used to capture tons of value in the 90s (when you bought a CD), now
  with streaming has very low value capture (music is almost free)

- Competition is inevitable
  - You don't have competition only if your idea is a bad one
  - If your competition has lock-in on the industry (e.g., distribution
    channels, exclusive contracts) that's a problem, if it's a startup go for it
  - You want to have a bias for markets that are not super crowded

- Often it makes sense to start targeting a small population, even if the TAM is
  big
  - You want to build a product that people love than people like

* You need to validate your idea
- How do you know know if your idea is good?
  - You can't solve this problem by thinking about it in a dark room
  - You need external feedback

- Who will use your product?
- Define your user
- Talk to people
- Interview customers
- Understand how they feel about this problem
- How are they solving the problem today?
  - Know what other products they use, where they fall short
  - If there is no solution they are using, probably there is no problem!

- "Would you use this if I built it?"
  - The answer is usually yes (since people tell you what you want to hear, and
    people want to be nice in a survey)
  - The answer is yes only if they actually pay for it ("would you pre-pay for
    it?")

- Figure out their pain points

* Your first idea is usually not perfect
- It's ok to pivot: it happens all the time, e.g.,
  - Mt. Gox was a platform for Magic the Gathering cards
  - Coinbase was a Bitcoin wallet
  - Circle originally a P2P payments product (like CashApp)
  - OpenSea was WifiCoin (pay other people's wifi with crypto)
  - DYdX (derivatives) was a web3 search engine
- Keep in mind the possibility of a pivot
- Have a strong bias toward reversible decisions

- The first idea gives you insights into the right way to solve a problem
  - Ideate quickly
  - Build quickly
  - Disprove assumptions quickly

- Do not hide your idea
  - Talk to everyone
