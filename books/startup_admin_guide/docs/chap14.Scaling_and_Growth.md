## Growth Strategies and Market Dynamics

* Moonshot vs a sure-win
- Moonshot = try to solve a problem that is kind of crazy, but if it works it's
  going to be absolutely massive
- Sure win = it's not a revolutionary idea but it's good business

- Startup returns are distributed as power-law
  - Almost all the returns come from the moonshot successes
  - The failure rate for moonshot are extremely high

- If you build a moonshot, people (talent and VCs) are excited and energized
  - Moonshots builds gravitational pull
  - Amazon: "people are going to buy everything on-line"
  - Solana: "the fastest blockchain"
  - OpenSea: NFTs were illiquid, create market to increase a liquidity for NFTs
  - Uniswap: create best way to trade tokens

- Sure things are not "venture scale"
  - VCs want to invest in moonshots

- "X on Y" startup ideas (e.g., "Uber in Argentina", "OpenSea for Cardano")
  - Moonshot: "do something nobody is doing"
  - X on Y: "do something somewhere no one else is doing it"
  - Because of the efficient market hypothesis (EMH), either X or Y is small
    otherwise somebody would have already done it

* Legacy companies lose to startups all the time
- Retail: Amazon vs Walmart
- Automotive: Tesla vs GM
- Media: Facebook / YouTube vs Fox
- Hospitality: Airbnb vs Marriott

- When you change the technology, you also need to change the human organization
  - E.g., for a Web3 CEO needs to be technical, COO is the one talking about
    money
  - Startups build the organization from the ground up to match the new needs

* Flywheel of innovation
- A solution starts off as a "worse" solution to a problem

- Infrastructure
- Killer apps
- User growth
- Capital and founders
- -> Investment in infrastructure (e.g., L2)

- Then the flywheel is kicked off and it reaches escape velocity and unstoppable
  feedback loop

- Crypto markets will eat the world
  - Decentralized computing
  - Open source dev infra
  - Creator economies
  - Social
