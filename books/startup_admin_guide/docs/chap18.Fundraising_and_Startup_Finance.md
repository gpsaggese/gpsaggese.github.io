## Pitching to Investors

- Startup founders are pitching all the times
  - To fund raise
  - To employees
  - To partners

* Before the meeting
- Try to build a relationship before pitching
- Fundraising should not be the first interaction
- Always try to get a warm intro
  - Much higher conversion rate
- Investors rarely take cold meetings
  - Crypto VCs even less since industry is full of noise, BS artist, scammers
- Use social proof (I went to Berkeley, I've worked with ...)

- VCs get tons of emails
  - You need to cut through the noise

- If you must go cold, try to engage
  - "I've read your Twitter", "I've read your paper"
  - Give enough personalization so it doesn't look like mass email

- Canvas all the funds that could be a fit for your startup
  - There are DBs with principals, what they invest in, ...

- Have an idea of who are prospective investors
  - Look at their portfolios, Crunchbase history
  - Most investors have specialization in certain areas

* How to ask for a warm intro
- Be direct
  - E.g., I'm doing this and that, can you do an intro for XYZ?"
  - Always provide a blurb (something you can copy-paste, don't play yourself up
    too much)
- Intros are typically double opt-in
  - You ask for an intro
  - The person sends the blurb, asks if it's ok to do an intro
  - Do the intro
- The more high-profile the person, the busier it is
  - Don't get upset if somebody doesn't respond
  - Try to keep meeting them
- The introduction is like "this person is an awesome guy, you should chat with
  him"
- Even VCs try to get intro to founders

* Practice pitch with friends / advisors
- Get honest feedback is difficult
- Most people don't like to tell you that things are bad
  - Ask them to be super mean, pick on everything, be tough
- Pitch on video (self-Zoom)
  - Wait a day to forget what you did!
  - Critique yourself
  - Take notes and improve

* Prepare your deck and materials
- Pretty much any presentation requires a deck

- For a seed round
  - Tell a story in your deck
  - Narrative is everything
  - What are you going to build?
  - Why it's going to be huge?

- Keep it short (shorter than you think, e.g., 10 slides)
  - A good idea is simple to explain

- Keep slides simple and uncluttered
  - Avoid wall of text, too much fine print

- The deck is not your pitch
  - It's just a visual aid / tool to augment your pitch
  - It should help to structure your pitch

- Build your deck as if your audience is stupid and easily distracted
  - Very easy language
  - Avoid context dependence for each slide (people fall asleep and wake up)

- Don't spend time explaining how cool crypto is
  - If you are pitching them, they should already be on-board

* Five things to convey

  1. Who you are
     - In a seed round, VCs invest in the founders and the team
  2. What you do
     - It shouldn't take 5 slides to set the context to explain what you are
       doing
  3. Why your startup will work
     - What's your edge
  4. Why it'll change everything
     - Why is it going to be a good deal?
  5. What you want from me
     - How much you want to raise?
     - Be direct

* Things you don't need to convey
- In a seed round, you don't need a monetization plan or token model
- The focus is "are you going to build something valuable?"
  - How to extract value comes later

- A valuation
  - You need to know how much you are raising
  - The market usually determines the valuation

- A TAM
  - Mostly TAM calculations in crypto are BS, it's just difficult to estimate

* The actual pitch
- Do not just read through your deck!
- Deck is a visual aid to amplify what you are saying
- You are not narrating your deck, you need to show who you are
- Feel free to skip slides, run quickly

- The point of a pitch is to persuade the investor, not to cover the pitch

- Don't try to impress with technical jargon
  - Over-complication dazzle investors
  - High-quality investors assume you can explain complicated concepts in a
    simple way
  - If you can't do it, you won't be able to do it to customers, employees

- Use charts and diagrams, make them simple

- If the deck requires attention to parse and decipher, it's distracting away
  from you, instead of being a visual aid

- Make your pitch as clear as possible
  - Teach the investors, don't just try to impress

- Sell your vision
  - Project enthusiasm, excitement
  - Show that you are the founder that can do it

* Pulling a round together
- You want to look busy and you are in high-demand
  - Signalling is half the game
- Investors are monkeys:
  - "Do you want this? Yes? I want it too"
- Something wants to talk
  - Saying "I'm free all day" is bad
  - I'm busy but I have a couple of slides

- Build the impression of scarcity
  - "I'm chatting with a lot of investors"
  - "Allocation in your round might be tight"
  - "My round is in high demand, you better act quickly"

- A lack of choosiness signals a lack of demand or a lack of experience

- If you promise someone something, your word should be ironclad
  - Investors talk
  - Reputations last a long time
  - When you are a founder, you are in the big league

- Unless you make a promise or sign a term sheet (not binding but a gentlemen
  agreement), everything is preliminary
  - People can be upset (for the right or the wrong reasons)

- Think in terms a pipeline
  - Reach out to investors you are less excited about first
  - Get some pitching reps in
  - Then go after the dream team
  - Contact slower-moving investors first (bigger, older firms)

- "I can't give you a response right now, but I will be able to in X days"
  - Be in control of the process

- Pursue a strong lead
  - A strong lead takes at least half a round
  - The strong lead set the terms and the valuation
  - The opposite is a party round (the responsibility to support you is diluted)

- A party round with small funds might be ok, since everybody is working hard

- How much you need to spend and to raise

- Every time you have a zero-sum decision, it's a negotiation point

- You need to always show strength

- Usually it takes 2-3 meetings to get to a term sheet

- Angels can do more work pound for pound than a big VC would do
- Sometimes angels are pain in the neck

* Valuation
- Founders want to maximize valuation (since it means less dilution for the money
  they give you)

- Vast majority of the startup (98-99%) doesn't see a value

- Almost always the VC that gives you the highest valuation is not the best VC
  to maximize the chances of success

- Valuation is not a grade, but it is a tool to maximize your changes of success

- It is super important to get the right persons at the table

- Once terms are in sight, you need to introduce deadlines and get things moving
  - Otherwise investors assume they can wait
  - Founders need to prod and get things closed
