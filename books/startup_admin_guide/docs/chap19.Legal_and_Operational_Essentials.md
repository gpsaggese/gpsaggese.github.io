## Regulatory Framework and Compliance

* Basics of regulations
- Rules that:
  - prohibit conduct (e.g., robbery) or
  - condition it on compliance with certain obligations (e.g., activities that
    you can carry out but you need to follow certain practices)

* Why do we need regulations?
- The belief is "The market cannot be left to its own devices"
- Reduce market power (e.g., monopolies)
- Facilitate competition (e.g., markets are open and new firms can enter and
  challenge the incompetents)
- Promote stability
- Attract investments
- Protect consumers
- Achieve governmental interests

* Who makes and enforces 
- US has regulations on both state and federal levels
- There are 3 branches of Government
  - Legislature branch (Congress) make laws
  - Executive enforces laws
  - Judiciary interprets laws

* Securities laws
- SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission) goals are:
  - protect investors
  - facilitate capital formation
  - maintain fair, orderly, and efficient markets

- In 1920 ("the roaring 20s") there was a huge bubble of:
  - Financial abuse
  - Information asymmetry (e.g., issuers of financial instruments vs purchasers)
- Federal securities laws originated in 1930 after the Great Depression

- SEC regulates securities and market actors (e.g., issuers, exchanges, advisers,
  brokers, dealers, investment firms)

* Crypto and securities laws
- Registered securities are subject to onerous requirements
  - E.g., traded on SEC-regulated venues, held by SEC-approved custodians
- Designation of a digital asset as a security is a death blow
  - Crypto industry spends time and money to avoid SEC regulation

* Anti-Money Laundering Laws
- Global framework for detecting and preventing criminals from using financial
  systems
- The focus is on financial intermediaries (e.g., bank, casino), you need to do
  KYC (Know-Your-Customers), e.g., understand source and purpose of transactions
  and report it to the Government
- The government doesn't have enough resources to monitor transactions, so it
  delegates this to intermediaries turning them into government agencies
  - The problem is that crypto replace all intermediaries with code
  - This still allows someone to monitor transactions but it's a big shift for
    the government
- Money transmitter = entity that takes custody of customer funds to keep safe or
  to transmit
  - E.g., Coinbase, Kraken, PayPal
  - They are all regulated and need to do KYC
